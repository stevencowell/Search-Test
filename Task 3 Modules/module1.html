1. WHS Legislation & Guidance

The foundation of any risk management process is understanding the legislative framework:

  • WHS Act 2011: Primary legislation requiring duty holders to eliminate or minimise risks to health and safety. Obligations apply to employers, workers and others.
  • WHS Regulations: Detailed requirements under the Act (e.g. licensing for high-risk work, plant operation standards).
  • Codes of Practice: Practical guides on how to achieve the standards set out in the Act and Regulations (e.g. Manual Handling, Hazardous Chemicals).
  • Australian Standards: Standards that may be mandatory (when referenced) or voluntary (e.g. AS/NZS ISO 31000:2018 Risk Management). These define best practice for processes and equipment.
  • S/NZS ISO 14001:2016 Environmental management systems: Guides development of site-specific Environmental Management Plans (EMPs).

2. Hazard vs. Risk

Hazard — any source or situation with potential to cause harm (e.g. slippery floor, unguarded machinery).

Risk — the likelihood and severity of harm arising from a hazard.

Key point: You must first identify hazards, then assess risks before applying controls.

3. Risk Assessment Process & Matrix

A systematic approach ensures all hazards are captured and controlled.

  1. Identify Hazards: Walk the site, consult workers, review incident reports.
  2. Assess Risks: Use a risk matrix to rate each hazard by likelihood and consequence.
  3. Implement Controls: Apply measures according to the hierarchy of controls.
  4. Record and Review: Document in a JSA or SWMS and monitor effectiveness.

4. Hierarchy of Controls

Controls must follow this sequence to be effective. Aim to use measures as high up the hierarchy as possible and combine controls when needed.

  1. Elimination — Remove the hazard entirely (e.g. cease use of a dangerous chemical).
  2. Substitution — Replace the hazard with a safer alternative (e.g. use a water‑based paint instead of a solvent‑based paint).
  3. Isolation — Separate people from the hazard (e.g. install physical barriers around an excavation or place noisy equipment in an enclosed room).
  4. Engineering Controls — Modify equipment or use mechanical devices to reduce exposure (e.g. machine guards, ventilation systems, lifting equipment).
  5. Administrative Controls — Change how people work by implementing safe work procedures, training, job rotation and warning signs.
  6. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) — Provide suitable PPE such as gloves, respirators, hard hats and hearing protection as the last line of defence when risks remain.

5. Duty of Care Responsibilities

You (Worker) Must:

  • Take reasonable care for your own health and safety.
  • Ensure your actions do not harm others.
  • Comply with instructions and safe work procedures.
  • Report hazards, incidents and injuries promptly.

Your Employer Must:

  • Provide and maintain a safe work environment.
  • Consult with workers on WHS matters.
  • Provide information, training and supervision.
  • Implement hazard controls according to the hierarchy.

6. Hazard Reporting Process

When you identify a hazard:

  1. Isolate/Control any immediate risk if safe to do so (e.g. erect barrier).
  2. Assess the hazard using likelihood/severity.
  3. Report to your supervisor or using site reporting system (JSA register).
  4. Record details in JSA/SWMS and follow up controls.
  5. Review after control implementation.

7. Safe Work Practices & Instructions

Examples of Safe Work Practices:

  • Following emergency procedures promptly.
  • Performing risk analysis before tasks.
  • Proper storage and removal of debris.
  • Using PPE as indicated (ear, eye, foot protection).

Key Documents:

  • Job Safety Analysis (JSA): Breaks tasks into steps, identifies hazards and controls.
  • Safety Data Sheet (SDS): Details safe handling, storage and disposal of hazardous materials.

Both JSA and SDS must be consulted and kept current.

8. Site Induction & Licensing

  • Site-Specific Induction before any work begins, covering hazards, emergency plans, and site rules.
  • Toolbox Talks to discuss daily hazards and controls.
  • Verify licences for high-risk work: forklift, confined space entry, working at heights.

9. PPE & Signage

PPE for Circular Saw Operation:

  • Steel cap boots
  • Safety glasses
  • Dust mask/respirator
  • Hearing protection

Purpose of Barricades & Signage:

  • Protect the general public and workers.
  • Comply with Australian Standards and WHS legislation.
  • Warn of hazards and isolate dangerous areas.

ACM stands for Asbestos Containing Material.
